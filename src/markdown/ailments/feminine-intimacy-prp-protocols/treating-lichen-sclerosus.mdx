# Treating Lichen Sclerosus with Feminine Intimacy PRP Protocols

Lichen sclerosus is a chronic inflammatory skin condition that most often affects the vulva, causing thin white patches, itching, and tearing that can interfere with comfort and intimacy.[1][2] This guide covers common symptoms, causes, and standard treatments, then explains how Feminine Intimacy PRP Protocols may support tissue health as an adjunct for select patients.[1][3] Evidence for PRP in vulvar conditions is still emerging, so it should be considered alongside proven therapies and ongoing monitoring.[4]

## Key takeaways

- Lichen sclerosus can cause persistent itching, fragile skin, and pain with sexual activity or urination, especially when fissures or tearing occur.[1][2]
- The condition is not contagious or sexually transmitted, and autoimmune or hormonal factors may contribute.[1][3]
- High-potency topical corticosteroids are first-line care and often require maintenance therapy with regular follow-up.[2][3]
- Feminine Intimacy PRP Protocols are not standard of care; they may support tissue quality for select patients, but evidence remains limited.[4]

## Symptoms and impact

Symptoms often include intense itching, burning, and discomfort that may worsen at night. The vulvar skin can appear thin, pale, or white and may bruise or tear easily. Some people develop fissures, small cuts, or bleeding after friction, and others experience pain with sex, tampon use, or pelvic exams.[1][2] Over time, scarring can cause changes in vulvar anatomy, including narrowing of the vaginal opening or adhesions that make intimacy more difficult.[2]

Some people notice painful urination if urine contacts irritated skin, and others report tearing or bleeding after wiping. The condition can also involve the perianal area, which may lead to discomfort with bowel movements or wiping. Persistent itching can disturb sleep and contribute to anxiety or low mood. Because symptoms can fluctuate, a symptom diary can help track triggers such as friction, certain fabrics, or hygiene products.[1][2]

Lichen sclerosus can be isolating because symptoms are intimate and sometimes misunderstood. People may avoid exercise, intimacy, or even routine activities for fear of flare-ups. If vaginal dryness or painful sex are part of your experience, you may also find it helpful to read [Treating Vaginal Dryness with Feminine Intimacy PRP Protocols](/procedures/feminine-intimacy-prp-protocols/treating-vaginal-dryness) and [Treating Dyspareunia with Feminine Intimacy PRP Protocols](/procedures/feminine-intimacy-prp-protocols/treating-dyspareunia).

Some individuals have minimal symptoms and notice skin changes only on exam, while others have significant discomfort and sleep disruption. Because symptoms can overlap with other vulvar conditions, a clinical evaluation is important for a clear diagnosis and an effective care plan.[1][3]

## Common causes and risk factors

The exact cause of lichen sclerosus is not fully understood. Current research suggests a combination of immune system changes, hormonal factors, and genetic susceptibility may play a role.[1][3] The condition is most common in postmenopausal people but can occur at any age, including in children. It is not caused by infection, sexual activity, or poor hygiene.[1]

Certain autoimmune conditions, such as thyroid disease, are more common in people with lichen sclerosus, which supports the theory of immune involvement.[3] Friction, chronic irritation, or local trauma can also trigger or worsen symptoms. Because there is no single cause, treatment focuses on controlling inflammation, protecting skin integrity, and preventing scarring.[2]

## Standard treatment options (non-promotional overview)

High-potency topical corticosteroid ointments are the first-line treatment for vulvar lichen sclerosus. A common approach is daily application for several weeks followed by a taper and long-term maintenance. This regimen reduces inflammation, improves symptoms, and lowers the risk of scarring when used correctly and monitored by a clinician.[2][3]

When symptoms persist despite optimized steroid therapy, other options may be considered. These can include topical calcineurin inhibitors for selected patients, supportive skin care with gentle emollients, and avoidance of irritants such as fragranced soaps or tight clothing.[2] If pain with sex is a major concern, pelvic floor physical therapy and lubricants can help address muscle tension and friction-related discomfort.

Long-term steroid use is generally safe when prescribed appropriately and monitored. Clinicians adjust potency and frequency to minimize side effects while keeping inflammation controlled.[2] Some patients benefit from education on gentle vulvar care, including using plain emollients, avoiding douches or fragranced wipes, and choosing breathable cotton underwear. These strategies reduce irritation and support the skin barrier between flares.

Regular follow-up is essential. Clinicians often recommend periodic exams to monitor disease control and check for suspicious changes, since lichen sclerosus is associated with a small but real risk of vulvar squamous cell carcinoma.[1][2] Biopsy may be recommended when the diagnosis is uncertain or when lesions change in appearance.

## How Feminine Intimacy PRP Protocols may help

Feminine Intimacy PRP Protocols use platelet-rich plasma derived from your own blood. PRP contains growth factors that may support tissue repair, collagen remodeling, and microcirculation. When injected into targeted vulvovaginal tissues, PRP is intended to promote healthier tissue quality and resilience.

For patients with lichen sclerosus who have persistent dryness, sensitivity, or micro-tearing despite standard care, PRP may be considered as an adjunct. It is not a substitute for topical corticosteroids or routine monitoring, but it may complement those treatments by supporting tissue hydration and elasticity. A consultation helps determine whether PRP aligns with your symptom pattern, medical history, and goals.

## Evidence and limitations

Evidence for PRP in vulvar conditions, including lichen sclerosus, is still limited. Studies are generally small and use different protocols, so results are difficult to compare.[4] Current clinical guidelines for lichen sclerosus continue to prioritize high-potency topical steroids and ongoing follow-up as the standard of care.[2][3]

It is also important to recognize that lichen sclerosus is a chronic condition. Even when symptoms improve, maintenance treatment and regular exams remain necessary to prevent flares and detect suspicious changes early.[1][2] PRP should be viewed as a supportive option rather than a cure, and it cannot remove the need for medical surveillance or proven therapies.

## Who may be a candidate and who should avoid treatment

You may be a candidate if you have a confirmed diagnosis, are stable on standard therapy, and still experience discomfort related to tissue quality or sensitivity. Ideal candidates are in overall good health, understand that PRP is an adjunct option, and are committed to ongoing follow-up for lichen sclerosus.

You may need to avoid or postpone PRP if you are pregnant, have an active infection or untreated flare, have a bleeding disorder, take certain anticoagulants, or have unexplained lesions that require biopsy. People with rapidly changing symptoms or new ulcerations should be evaluated by a gynecologist or dermatologist before any elective treatment.[1][2]

## What to expect (timeline, sessions, aftercare)

A visit typically starts with a detailed history, symptom review, and vulvar exam. If you have not had a recent evaluation, your clinician may recommend coordination with your gynecologist or dermatologist to confirm diagnosis and ensure appropriate baseline treatment.

For PRP, a small blood sample is drawn and processed to isolate platelet-rich plasma. The PRP is then injected into targeted tissues. The appointment is usually under an hour, and topical numbing can be used for comfort. Treatment plans vary, but some patients choose a series of sessions spaced a few weeks apart, followed by reassessment.

Changes in tissue quality may take several weeks to appear, with continued improvement over a few months as collagen remodeling occurs. Aftercare usually includes gentle hygiene, avoiding irritants, and temporary abstinence from sexual activity or strenuous exercise as directed. Standard lichen sclerosus therapy should continue unless your clinician advises otherwise.

## Risks and side effects

Because PRP uses your own blood, allergic reactions are unlikely. Temporary soreness, swelling, bruising, or light spotting can occur after injections. There is also a small risk of infection or irritation, which is why sterile technique and careful screening are essential.

It is equally important to avoid delaying proven care. If symptoms worsen, new lesions develop, or bleeding persists, prompt medical evaluation is needed. PRP should complement, not replace, evidence-based management and surveillance for lichen sclerosus.[2]

## FAQs

### What is lichen sclerosus?
Lichen sclerosus is a chronic inflammatory skin condition that most often affects the vulva, causing thin white patches, itching, and skin fragility.[1][2]

### Is lichen sclerosus contagious or an STI?
No. Lichen sclerosus is not contagious and is not caused by sexual activity or poor hygiene.[1]

### What is the first-line treatment?
High-potency topical corticosteroid ointments are the standard first-line therapy, often used on a tapering schedule with long-term maintenance.[2][3]

### Do I need a biopsy or regular monitoring?
Clinicians often recommend biopsy to confirm the diagnosis or to evaluate new or changing lesions, and regular follow-up helps monitor for scarring or cancer risk.[1][2]

### Can PRP replace steroid therapy?
No. PRP is an adjunct option that may support tissue quality, but it does not replace steroid therapy or ongoing monitoring.[2][4]

### How long does treatment take?
Lichen sclerosus is chronic, so management is long term. Many people need maintenance therapy and periodic follow-up to keep symptoms controlled.[2][3]
